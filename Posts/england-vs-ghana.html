England dominated possession and territory but couldn't break down a well-organized Ghana side, with Harry Kane's late miss (blazing over an open goal after O'Reilly's header bounced off the crossbar) summing up a frustrating night in front of goal. The match will be remembered more for two separate refereeing incidents that both went in England's favor, with Ghana boss Carlos Queiroz furious afterward, joking that "VAR went for a coffee."

🟥 Controversy 1: foul awarded the wrong way (67')
Jordan Pickford rushed out of his box and collided with Ghana's Prince Adu after the ball had already gone past him. In real time it looked clearly like a foul committed by Pickford. Instead, referee Said Martinez astonishingly awarded the free kick to England, ruling that Pickford had been the one fouled. ESPN's own VAR review column confirmed Pickford was fortunate, noting any final touch on the ball by Adu before the collision should have resulted in a red card for the England goalkeeper. This wasn't reviewed on-field despite the clear visual evidence available - a genuine missed opportunity for VAR to correct an obvious error.
🟥 Controversy 2: penalty appeal waved away (79')
Mohammed Fatawu played Adu through with a clever pass, and as he raced into the box, Ezri Konsa appeared to bring him down before the ball ran loose. The referee waved away Ghana's penalty appeal without sending the decision to review. Former Premier League assistant referee Darren Cann told the BBC it was a penalty, or at minimum should have been reviewed by VAR. Ghana manager Carlos Queiroz was blunt afterward, calling it "a clear penalty against England" and openly questioning whether VAR was even operating at the time. Two major incidents in the same match, both going the same way, is a pattern worth genuine scrutiny rather than dismissing as coincidence.
🔍 Tactical Review

England's approach was patient build-up against a deep, disciplined Ghana low block - 64% possession and 15 shots reflects territorial control, but Ghana's organization (and no shortage of fortune) meant clear sight-of-goal moments were rare. Declan Rice's long-range effort and Jude Bellingham's blocked shot were the closest things to real first-half quality.

Ghana's gameplan was exactly what's needed against a stronger opponent - stay compact, deny space, and look to hit on the counter through Adu and Ayew's energy. It worked well enough to secure a point that, combined with their opening win over Panama, leaves them well-placed for qualification regardless of the refereeing controversy.

The key tactical moment was less about formations and more about game management - Ghana's discipline in defending set pieces and crosses (England's strongest avenue per pre-match data) limited the one area where England were statistically most dangerous on Matchday 1.
